Risk Fundamentals

Every security decision starts here
TermDefinitionExam Focus
RiskThe likelihood that a threat will exploit a vulnerability, resulting in harm to an asset. Expressed as: Risk = Likelihood × Impact.Risk is a combination of both factors — not just likelihood or just impact alone.
ImpactThe magnitude of harm if a risk materializes — financial loss, operational disruption, data breach, reputational damage, or regulatory penalties.High impact + high likelihood = highest priority risk.

Threat types

Threat TypeDescriptionExamples
Malicious human threatAn intentional act by a person aiming to cause harm — insider or outsider.Hacker, disgruntled employee planting a logic bomb, nation-state actor exfiltrating data.
Accidental human threatUnintentional harm caused by a person without malicious intent.Employee misconfiguring a firewall rule, accidentally emailing PII to the wrong address, dropping a laptop.
Environmental threatNatural or physical events outside human control that can damage systems or data.Earthquake, flood, hurricane, fire, power outage, extreme temperature causing hardware failure.

Threat assessment & risk identification

  • Threat assessment — the process of identifying and evaluating threats relevant to an organization: which actors are likely to target you, with what capabilities, using which vectors. Feeds directly into risk identification.
  • Risk identification — cataloging all risks the organization faces by pairing threats with the vulnerabilities they could exploit across all assets. The output is the input to assessment and the risk register.
Exam cue: Threat assessment asks "who/what could hurt us?" Risk identification asks "what could go wrong with our assets?" — two separate steps in the risk management lifecycle.

Know what you're classifying

Risk Management

The ongoing process — not a one-time event

Risk management is the continuous process of identifying, assessing, treating, and monitoring risks to keep them within acceptable levels. It wraps around everything else in this chapter.

The three risk states

Risk StateDefinitionWhen It Applies
Inherent riskThe level of risk that exists before any controls are applied. The raw, unmitigated exposure.Used as the starting baseline when entering a risk into the register.
Residual riskThe risk that remains after controls are applied. No control eliminates risk completely — residual risk must be accepted, transferred, or further mitigated.Compared to risk appetite to determine if additional treatment is needed.
Control riskThe risk that an existing control will fail or be circumvented, allowing the underlying risk to materialize anyway.Considered when evaluating the reliability of a control during a risk assessment.

Risk appetite

Risk appetite is the amount of risk an organization is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives. It reflects strategy and culture. Three postures:

Expansionary

High risk appetite. The organization accepts significant risk to pursue growth, innovation, or competitive advantage. Startups and high-growth companies often operate here.

Neutral

Balanced risk appetite. The organization accepts risk proportional to the expected return. Risks are evaluated case-by-case without a strong bias toward caution or aggression.

Conservative

Low risk appetite. The organization minimizes risk exposure even at the cost of slower growth. Government agencies, healthcare, and financial institutions typically operate here.

Risk tolerance & continuous risk assessment

  • Risk tolerance — the acceptable variation around the risk appetite. If appetite is "no more than 1% data-breach probability per year," tolerance defines how far above that threshold action is required (e.g., 1.5% triggers a mitigation project). It is the operational boundary.
  • Continuous risk assessment — an ongoing, real-time approach to risk assessment rather than a periodic point-in-time audit. Threat landscapes, assets, and controls change constantly; continuous assessment surfaces new risks before they materialize. Enabled by SIEM, threat intelligence feeds, and automated vulnerability scanning.
Appetite vs. tolerance: Risk appetite is the strategic goal ("how much we want to take on"); risk tolerance is the operational limit ("how far we'll deviate before acting"). A company may have a conservative appetite but a tolerance that permits small exceptions with documented approval.

Assets & Quantitative Risk Analysis

Turn risk into dollar figures — the formulas the exam tests directly

Asset fundamentals

TermDefinition
AssetAnything of value the organization needs to protect: hardware, software, data, intellectual property, personnel, facilities, or reputation.
Asset value (AV)The dollar (or equivalent monetary) value assigned to an asset — replacement cost, market value, or business impact of losing it. AV is the starting point for every quantitative risk calculation.
Risk control assessmentEvaluating the effectiveness of a specific control against a specific risk — does the control actually reduce likelihood or impact, and by how much?
Risk control self-assessmentA structured process where the business unit that owns the risk assesses its own controls rather than relying solely on an independent audit. Can be quantitative or qualitative.

The quantitative formula chain

VariableFull NameDefinitionFormula
AVAsset ValueDollar value of the asset being evaluated.— (input, set by asset inventory)
EFExposure FactorPercentage of the asset value expected to be lost in a single incident. Expressed as a decimal (0.0–1.0).— (input, estimated per threat)
SLESingle Loss ExpectancyExpected dollar loss from one occurrence of the risk.SLE = AV × EF
AROAnnualized Rate of OccurrenceExpected number of times the risk will occur per year. Can be a fraction (0.1 = once every 10 years).— (input, from historical data or estimates)
ALEAnnualized Loss ExpectancyExpected total dollar loss per year from this risk. This is the number to compare against safeguard cost.ALE = SLE × ARO

Impact & risk reporting

  • Impact — in the context of quantitative analysis, impact is operationalized as SLE: the dollar consequence of one event. Qualitatively it may be rated High/Medium/Low. Either way, it answers: "how bad is it if this happens?"
  • Risk reporting — communicating identified risks, their quantified values, current treatment status, and trends to management and stakeholders. Risk reports drive resource allocation and executive decision-making.
Safeguard formula: A control is worth buying if (ALEbefore − ALEafter) − Annual Safeguard Cost > 0. The difference between pre- and post-control ALE is the value the safeguard delivers. The exam may present this as a numeric scenario.

Qualitative Risk Analysis

When numbers aren't available — or aren't the point

Qualitative risk analysis replaces dollar calculations with descriptive ratings. Likelihood and impact are each scored Low / Medium / High (or on a 1–5 scale). The resulting risk level is determined by crossing the two scores on a risk matrix. Used when quantitative data is unavailable, unreliable, or when you need fast stakeholder communication.

MethodHow It WorksOutputBest For
QuantitativeAssigns monetary values; uses AV, EF, SLE, ARO, ALE formulasDollar amounts (ALE)Justifying specific control spend; insurance; executive reporting
QualitativeRates likelihood and impact with descriptive levels; plots on a risk matrixRisk level (High / Med / Low)Fast prioritization; broad risk inventories; scenarios without reliable data

Risk matrix (heat map)

The risk matrix maps likelihood (horizontal axis) against impact (vertical axis). Each cell represents the combined risk level for that combination. High-impact + high-likelihood risks land in the top-right "red zone" and demand immediate treatment.

Exam cue: "Using a heat map to rate risks as High/Medium/Low" = qualitative analysis. "Calculating ALE using SLE × ARO" = quantitative analysis. Both feed the risk register.

Risk Register

The living document of every identified risk

A risk register is a formal, structured document (often a spreadsheet or GRC tool) that catalogs every identified risk in the organization. It is continuously updated as risks are identified, treated, change status, or close. It is the primary output of risk assessment and the input to risk treatment decisions.

FieldWhat Goes Here
Risk IDUnique identifier (R-001, R-002…) for tracking and referencing.
Risk descriptionClear statement of the risk: what could happen, to which asset, due to which threat/vulnerability.
LikelihoodQualitative (H/M/L) or quantitative (ARO) rating of how often this risk may materialize.
ImpactQualitative (H/M/L) or quantitative (SLE) rating of the harm if it does.
Risk levelCombined rating from the risk matrix — drives prioritization.
Current controlsExisting countermeasures already reducing this risk (what's already in place).
Risk response / treatmentThe chosen strategy: Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate, or Accept.
Residual riskRisk level after controls are applied — what the organization is living with.
Risk ownerThe person or role accountable for managing this risk.
Status / review dateOpen, closed, in treatment — and when it was last reviewed.

Risk response strategies

StrategyDefinitionExample
AvoidEliminate the risk entirely by stopping the risky activity. No activity = no exposure.Decide not to build a public-facing service that would introduce the risk.
Transfer (Share)Shift the financial impact to a third party, typically through insurance or a contract.Purchase cyber liability insurance; include indemnification clauses with a cloud vendor.
MitigateReduce the likelihood or impact of the risk to an acceptable level using controls.Patch the vulnerability, deploy a WAF, implement MFA.
AcceptAcknowledge the risk and choose to take no additional action — the residual risk is within appetite. Must be a documented, conscious decision.A low-impact/low-likelihood risk whose mitigation cost exceeds its ALE.
Accept ≠ ignore: Accepting a risk requires a formal documented sign-off from the appropriate risk owner. An undocumented assumption is not "acceptance" — it is negligence.

Supply Chain Risk

Third parties extend your attack surface

The supply chain encompasses every vendor, supplier, contractor, MSP, and open-source dependency the organization relies on. A compromise anywhere in the chain can cascade into your environment — through trusted software update mechanisms, shared infrastructure, or privileged vendor access.

  • Software supply chain — malicious code injected into a widely-used library or build pipeline (e.g., SolarWinds, XZ Utils backdoor). Every dependency is a potential entry point.
  • Hardware supply chain — counterfeit or tampered components inserted during manufacturing or shipping.
  • Service supply chain — a managed service provider (MSP) or cloud vendor with privileged access to your systems.
  • Mitigations — vendor risk assessments, contractual security requirements (SLAs, right-to-audit clauses), software composition analysis (SCA) for dependency scanning, code signing verification for updates.
Exam cue: "The attacker compromised the company through its IT management vendor" = supply chain attack via MSP. See also: Chapter 6 — Threat Vectors.

Vulnerability Assessment

Find your weaknesses before attackers do

A vulnerability assessment is a systematic review of an environment to identify security weaknesses. It stops at identification and reporting — unlike a penetration test, it does not attempt to exploit what it finds. Output: a prioritized list of vulnerabilities with remediation guidance.

Network scanner types — each does something distinct

Scanner / Scan TypeWhat It DoesExam Detail
Network scannerBroad term for tools that probe a network to discover hosts, open ports, running services, and potential vulnerabilities. Umbrella category for the types below.Nmap is the canonical network scanner — appears by name on the exam.
ARP ping scanSends ARP requests to each IP in a subnet to discover which hosts are alive. ARP operates at Layer 2 — works only on the local network segment. Cannot cross routers.Used first in a local-network assessment to enumerate live hosts before port scanning.
SYN stealth scanSends a TCP SYN packet (start of the three-way handshake) but never completes the connection — sends RST after receiving SYN-ACK. Also called a half-open scan. Stealthier than a full-connect scan because many older logging systems only record completed connections."Half-open" or "stealth" scan = SYN scan. Nmap flag: -sS.
Port scanProbes each port on a target host to determine whether it is open (service listening), closed (no service, RST returned), or filtered (firewall drops packets, no response).Three states: open, closed, filtered. Filtered = firewall likely present.
Service scanAttempts to identify the exact application and version running on each open port by sending probes and analyzing banner responses. Goes beyond "port 80 is open" to "Apache 2.4.51 is running on port 80."Used to match running services against CVE databases. Nmap flag: -sV.
OS detectionAnalyzes subtle differences in how the target's TCP/IP stack responds to crafted probes (TTL, window size, TCP options) to fingerprint the operating system and version.OS fingerprinting guides which exploits are applicable. Nmap flag: -O.
Vulnerability scannerCombines the results of port, service, and OS scans with a database of known vulnerabilities (CVEs) to identify which specific weaknesses likely exist on discovered targets.Nessus is the exam's canonical vulnerability scanner. Output includes severity ratings mapped to CVSS scores.
Configuration compliance scannerCompares a system's actual configuration against a known-good baseline (CIS Benchmark, STIG, PCI DSS requirements). Identifies configuration drift — settings that deviate from policy.Distinct from a vulnerability scanner: it checks settings, not software CVEs.

CVE · CVSS · SCAP

The standardized language of vulnerabilities
StandardFull NameWhat It IsExam Role
CVECommon Vulnerabilities and ExposuresA standardized identifier (e.g., CVE-2021-44228) for a publicly known vulnerability. Maintained by MITRE. Each CVE has a unique ID, description, and references.CVEs appear in scan reports to identify which specific flaw was found.
CVSSCommon Vulnerability Scoring SystemA numerical severity score from 0.0 to 10.0 (None → Low → Medium → High → Critical) calculated from base metrics: attack vector, complexity, privileges required, user interaction, and impact on CIA.Used to prioritize remediation — Critical (9.0–10.0) first. CVSS ≠ CVE: CVE names the flaw; CVSS scores its severity.
SCAPSecurity Content Automation ProtocolAn NIST framework of standards (including CVE, CVSS, CCE, XCCDF, OVAL) that automates vulnerability management, configuration compliance checking, and security measurement across an enterprise.SCAP is the automation framework that ties CVE and CVSS together with machine-readable content. Configuration compliance scanners often run SCAP content.

Vulnerability information sources

  • NVD (National Vulnerability Database) — NIST-maintained database of CVEs enriched with CVSS scores, affected products, and remediation references. The primary authoritative source.
  • Vendor advisories — Microsoft Patch Tuesday, Cisco PSIRT, Red Hat Security Advisories. First to publish details on flaws in their own products.
  • CERT/CC — Carnegie Mellon's coordination center; publishes vulnerability notes and coordinates disclosure.
  • Exploit databases — Exploit-DB, Rapid7 Metasploit modules; show what public exploit code exists for known CVEs (elevates priority).
  • Threat intelligence feeds — STIX/TAXII feeds, ISACs, commercial feeds that include emerging CVEs before they are widely published.

Scan Credential Types

Two fundamentally different views of a target

Credentialed scan

The scanner logs into the target system using valid credentials (admin account, SSH key, WMI access). It can read installed software, patch levels, registry keys, running processes, and configuration — a deep, accurate internal view.

Pros: Far fewer false positives; detects vulnerabilities invisible from the network (e.g., an unpatched library used only internally).

Cons: Requires managing credentials securely; a compromised scan account is a risk.

Non-credentialed scan

The scanner probes the target from the outside only — no login. It fingerprints services via network responses and banner grabbing. Simulates what an external attacker would see.

Pros: No credentials needed; shows attacker's perspective; faster to set up.

Cons: Higher false positive and false negative rates; cannot see inside the OS.

CredentialedNon-credentialed
ViewInside the OS and application layerOutside — network-facing services only
False positivesLowHigher
False negativesLowHigher (misses internal-only vulns)
SimulatesInsider / post-authentication attackerExternal unauthenticated attacker
Use caseCompliance scans, patch verification, internal assessmentsPerimeter assessments, simulating attacker perspective
False positive vs. false negative: A false positive is a reported vulnerability that doesn't actually exist (wastes remediation time). A false negative is a real vulnerability the scanner missed (the dangerous one — creates a false sense of security). Credentialed scans reduce both.

Penetration Testing

Authorized attack simulation — authorization is everything

A penetration test goes beyond a vulnerability assessment: it actively attempts to exploit discovered vulnerabilities to demonstrate real-world impact. Always requires written authorization before starting.

Penetration test types

TypeWhat It TestsHow It Works
Physical penetration testPhysical security controls — badge readers, locked doors, tailgating procedures, guard alertness, CCTV coverage.Testers attempt to gain unauthorized physical access to a facility through social engineering, bypassing access controls, or exploiting gaps in physical procedures.
Offensive (red team)The organization's full defensive capability — can attackers get in, move laterally, and reach high-value targets undetected?A red team simulates a real-world attacker using all available techniques against live production systems over an extended engagement.
Defensive (blue team)The detection and response capabilities of the security operations team.The blue team monitors and responds to simulated attacks. The test evaluates whether defenders can detect, contain, and eradicate a real intrusion.
Integrated (purple team)Both offensive and defensive capabilities simultaneously — and the collaboration between them.Red and blue teams work together (or alternate roles) in real time, immediately sharing what attacks were used and whether they were detected. Accelerates improvement.

Rules of engagement (ROE)

The rules of engagement are a written agreement between the tester and the client that defines the legal and operational boundaries of the test. The ROE must be signed and in place before any testing begins.

  • Scope — exactly which IP ranges, hosts, applications, or facilities are in scope (and which are explicitly out of scope).
  • Timing — permitted testing windows (e.g., only between 10 p.m. and 6 a.m. to avoid production impact).
  • Techniques — which attack techniques are permitted (e.g., social engineering allowed vs. prohibited; DoS testing explicitly prohibited).
  • Communication — emergency contact procedures if a tester discovers a critical vulnerability or causes unintended disruption.
  • Data handling — how sensitive data encountered during testing must be protected and disposed of.
No authorization = criminal activity. A penetration test without written authorization is indistinguishable from an actual attack under computer crime law. The ROE is what makes testing legal.

Bug bounty programs

A bug bounty is a public or private program in which an organization invites independent researchers to find and responsibly disclose security vulnerabilities in exchange for monetary rewards. Effectively outsources continuous security testing to a global pool of researchers under defined rules.

Tester's Level of Environment Knowledge

What the tester knows shapes what the test simulates
Knowledge LevelCommon NameWhat the Tester KnowsSimulates
Unknown environmentBlack-box testNothing — only the organization's public name (and possibly a general IP range). No internal knowledge, no credentials, no architecture diagrams.An external attacker with no prior knowledge. The most realistic simulation of an opportunistic external attack.
Known environmentWhite-box testEverything — full source code, network diagrams, architecture documentation, credentials, and system configurations are provided.An insider or an attacker who has already done extensive reconnaissance. Most thorough — finds the most vulnerabilities because nothing is hidden.

Exam cue: "The tester was given network diagrams and a standard user account" = gray-box. "The tester was given full source code and admin access" = white-box. "The tester was only told the company name" = black-box.

Reconnaissance & Footprinting

Phase 1 — know your target before touching it
TechniqueDefinitionMethods / Tools
Passive reconnaissanceGathering information about a target without directly interacting with its systems. Leaves no footprint on the target. Uses only publicly available information.WHOIS, DNS lookups, Google dorking, LinkedIn, Shodan, certificate transparency logs, job postings, OSINT frameworks.
Network reconnaissanceActive probing of the target's network — directly interacting with systems to map hosts, ports, and services. Will appear in the target's logs.Ping sweeps, ARP scans, port scans (nmap), traceroute. Used in the scanning phase.
Network footprintingBuilding a comprehensive map of the target's network topology: IP ranges, subnets, DNS records, ASN, routing paths, and publicly visible hosts.WHOIS, nslookup/dig, dnsenum, traceroute, BGP looking glass. Primarily passive.
FingerprintingIdentifying the exact software, version, and configuration of a service by analyzing its responses, banners, or behavior.Banner grabbing (netcat, cURL), HTTP header analysis, service probing (nmap -sV).
OS fingerprintingSpecifically identifying the operating system of a target host by analyzing TCP/IP stack behavior: TTL values, window size, TCP options, and ICMP responses.Active: nmap -O, hping. Passive: p0f (analyzes traffic without sending probes).

Penetration Test Phases & Techniques

What happens after you get in

Post-exploitation techniques — each term is distinct

TechniqueDefinitionExam Cue
PersistenceMaintaining access across system reboots, logouts, or other interruptions. The attacker installs a backdoor, creates a rogue service, adds a scheduled task, or creates new admin accounts so they don't have to re-exploit."Survived a reboot" or "continued access even after the initial vulnerability was patched" = persistence.
Privilege escalationMoving from a lower-privilege account to a higher-privilege one on the same system — e.g., from a standard user to local administrator, or from local admin to SYSTEM/root.Vertical movement within a single host. Enables access to protected data and the ability to install tools.
Vertical movementThe same concept as privilege escalation — moving up the privilege hierarchy on a host or in a domain. "Vertical" because it moves up the privilege ladder on one system.On the Security+ exam, vertical movement and privilege escalation refer to the same concept.
Lateral movementMoving horizontally across the network — from one compromised host to other hosts at a similar privilege level. Attacker uses credentials, sessions, or trust relationships to access additional machines.Techniques: pass-the-hash, pass-the-ticket, RDP with stolen creds, SMB exploitation. "Compromised multiple workstations from the first beachhead" = lateral movement.
PivotingUsing a compromised host as a relay point to reach network segments that are otherwise inaccessible to the attacker — such as an internal VLAN behind a firewall. The compromised host acts as a proxy or tunnel."Used the compromised web server to attack the database server on an isolated VLAN" = pivoting. Distinct from lateral movement: pivoting crosses network boundaries, lateral movement stays within one segment.
CleanupThe final phase — removing all tools, backdoors, created accounts, and logs generated during testing to return the system to its pre-test state. Required by the rules of engagement and professional ethics.Cleanup is the attacker's equivalent of "covering tracks" — in pentesting it is a required deliverable.

Packet Analysis Tools

Capturing, reading, and replaying network traffic
Tool / ConceptWhat It DoesKey Detail
Packet captureThe process of intercepting and recording raw network packets as they traverse a network interface. Stored in pcap (packet capture) format.Requires promiscuous mode on the NIC (or a SPAN port / network tap). Contains full packet contents including payloads.
Protocol analyzerSoftware that reads and decodes captured packets to make them human-readable — dissecting headers, protocols, and payloads across all OSI layers.Wireshark is the canonical GUI protocol analyzer on the exam. tcpdump is the CLI equivalent.
Packet replayRe-transmitting previously captured packets onto the network — used to test how systems respond to specific traffic patterns or to reproduce conditions that triggered an event.Useful for testing IDS/IPS rules, application behavior, or firewall configurations without needing a live attacker.
tcpreplayReplays captured pcap files onto the network at controlled speeds. The primary tool for packet replay in assessments.Works in conjunction with tcpprep and tcprewrite. Can replay at original timing or accelerated rates.
tcpdumpCommand-line packet capture and analysis tool. Captures live traffic or reads pcap files, filtered by BPF (Berkeley Packet Filter) expressions.The go-to CLI tool for network capture on Linux/Unix. Example: tcpdump -i eth0 port 80 -w capture.pcap.
tcpprepPreprocesses pcap files to classify packets as "client" or "server" traffic before replay with tcpreplay. Required when replaying two-sided conversations.Part of the tcpreplay suite. Used to correctly split bidirectional traffic for replay on dual-interface setups.
tcprewriteModifies pcap files before replay — changes source/destination IP addresses, MAC addresses, port numbers, or checksums to adapt captures to a new test environment.Also part of the tcpreplay suite. Essential when replaying captures in a different IP address space than the original.
NetFlowA Cisco-originated protocol (now standardized as IPFIX) that collects flow metadata from routers and switches: source/destination IP, port, protocol, packet count, byte count, and timestamps — without capturing full packet payloads.NetFlow = traffic statistics. Protocol analyzer = full packet contents. NetFlow is used for traffic analysis, capacity planning, anomaly detection, and security monitoring at scale.
Exam distinction: NetFlow records who talked to whom, when, and how much — no payloads. A protocol analyzer (Wireshark/tcpdump) captures everything including the contents of packets. For detecting anomalous patterns across an entire network, NetFlow scales better. For forensic examination of specific traffic, use a full packet capture.

Remediation Controls

How you fix what scanning and testing finds
Control / ActionDefinitionExam Detail
PatchingApplying vendor-released software updates that fix known vulnerabilities. The primary and most direct remediation for software CVEs.Patching should be prioritized by CVSS score and exploitability. Unpatched systems are the leading root cause of breaches. Patch management includes testing patches before deployment.
Compensating controlAn alternative control used when the preferred or primary control cannot be implemented — provides equivalent (or partial) protection through a different mechanism.Classic exam scenario: "The legacy system cannot be patched — what compensating control would you apply?" Answers: network segmentation, WAF, enhanced monitoring, disabling the vulnerable feature.
SegmentationDividing the network into isolated zones so that a compromise in one segment cannot directly reach others. Limits lateral movement and reduces blast radius.VLANs, firewall DMZ zones, microsegmentation in cloud environments. "Contains the attack to one segment" = segmentation worked.
ExceptionA formally documented waiver granting an exemption from a security policy or control requirement for a specific system or timeframe. Requires risk-owner sign-off and a defined review date.An exception is not a permanent state — it must have an expiration or remediation plan. Undocumented exceptions are policy violations.

Security Frameworks

Structured guidance that turns policy into practice

A framework is a structured set of guidelines, best practices, and controls that organizations use to build and maintain a security program. Frameworks are not laws — they are voluntary reference structures (unless contractually or regulatorily required).

ISO standards — each covers a different scope

StandardNameWhat It GovernsKey Fact
ISO 27001Information Security Management System (ISMS)Requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an ISMS. The certifiable standard — organizations can receive an ISO 27001 certification after a third-party audit.The only certifiable ISO security standard in this set. Defines the what (requirements), not the how.
ISO 27002Code of Practice for Information Security ControlsImplementation guidance for the controls referenced in ISO 27001 Annex A. Provides detailed recommendations on how to implement each control.Companion to 27001 — 27001 says what controls are needed; 27002 explains how to implement them.
ISO 27701Privacy Information Management System (PIMS)Extends ISO 27001 and 27002 to address privacy and the protection of personally identifiable information (PII). Aligns with GDPR principles.An extension to 27001, not a standalone standard. An organization needs 27001 first, then adds 27701 for privacy.
ISO 31000Risk Management — GuidelinesPrinciples, framework, and process for risk management across all types of risk — not limited to information security. A general risk management standard applicable to any industry.Not IT-specific. Broader than 27001. Provides the risk management philosophy that the security-specific standards build on.

NIST frameworks

FrameworkFull NamePurpose & Structure
NIST SP 800-37Risk Management Framework (RMF)A 7-step process for managing security and privacy risk in federal information systems: Prepare → Categorize → Select → Implement → Assess → Authorize → Monitor. Mandated for US federal agencies under FISMA.
NIST CSFCybersecurity FrameworkA voluntary framework for private-sector organizations organized around five core functions: Identify → Protect → Detect → Respond → Recover. Designed to help organizations manage and reduce cybersecurity risk using a common language.

NIST SP 800-37 RMF — the 7 steps

#StepWhat Happens
1PrepareEstablish organizational context, risk tolerance, and resources for the RMF process.
2CategorizeClassify the information system and its data by impact level (Low / Moderate / High) per FIPS 199.
3SelectChoose a baseline set of security controls from NIST SP 800-53 appropriate for the impact level.
4ImplementDeploy the selected controls and document how they are implemented.
5AssessIndependently evaluate whether controls are implemented correctly and operating effectively.
6AuthorizeA senior official makes a risk-based authorization decision: ATO (Authority to Operate) or denial.
7MonitorContinuously track control effectiveness, changes, and new risks — feeds back into the cycle.

Reference architecture

A reference architecture is a standardized, pre-built template for implementing security controls within a specific technology context (cloud, ICS, mobile, etc.). Rather than designing from scratch, organizations adopt a reference architecture as the starting point and customize it to their environment. Major examples include the NIST Cybersecurity Framework reference implementations, CISA Zero Trust reference architecture, and cloud-provider security landing zones.

Audits & Assessments

Verify that controls exist, are implemented, and work
ActivityDefinitionKey Distinction
AssessmentA structured evaluation of a system, process, or control set to determine its security posture — typically against a defined standard or baseline. Less formal than an audit; may be internal.Assessments evaluate; audits verify with formal evidence and attestation.
AuditA formal, evidence-based review to verify that defined controls are in place and operating effectively. Produces a formal report with findings, evidence, and conclusions.Audits have defined scope, criteria, evidence requirements, and formal conclusions.
Internal auditAn audit conducted by the organization's own internal audit team. Provides ongoing assurance and process improvement without the cost of external auditors.Internal auditors report to the audit committee / board to maintain independence from operations. Less trusted externally than external audits.
External auditAn audit conducted by an independent third party outside the organization. Required for compliance certifications (ISO 27001, SOC 2, PCI DSS), regulatory submissions, or when customers demand independent assurance.External audits are more credible to stakeholders. More expensive. Required for certifiable standards.
Gap analysisComparing the organization's current security state against a desired target state (a standard, framework, or best practice) to identify what is missing or non-compliant.Output is a list of gaps with prioritized remediation recommendations. Precedes an implementation project, not the audit of a completed one.
AttestationA formal, signed declaration by an authorized party (management, a CISO, or an auditor) that specified controls are in place and operating as described. Creates legal accountability.Attestation is a statement of assurance — signing off that controls exist and work. Distinct from just running a scan. Required in SOC 2 reports and regulatory filings.
Assessment vs. audit vs. gap analysis: Gap analysis asks "where are we vs. where we need to be?" An assessment asks "how strong are our current controls?" An audit asks "can we formally prove the controls operate?" — with documented evidence and a signed conclusion.
