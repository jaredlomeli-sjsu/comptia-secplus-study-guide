Physical Security Controls

If an attacker can touch the hardware, all digital controls can fail

Physical security is the first and most fundamental layer of protection. No amount of encryption or firewalls prevents an attacker who can walk up to a server, steal a laptop, or cut a power line. Physical controls fall into the same categories as any security control — preventive, detective, deterrent, and corrective — but they protect the physical environment rather than digital assets.

ControlTypeHow It Works
Access badgePreventive / TechnicalRFID or smart-card credential granting entry to secured areas when presented to a reader. Tied to identity management — deactivated instantly when an employee leaves.
Security guardsPreventive / Detective / DeterrentHuman personnel who deter threats, verify identities, respond to incidents, and make judgment calls. The most flexible physical control — can adapt to novel situations.
Video surveillance (CCTV)Detective / DeterrentClosed-circuit cameras that record and monitor spaces. Acts as a deterrent when visible; provides forensic evidence after an incident.
LightingDeterrent / PreventiveEliminates dark areas around facilities to deter unauthorized access and improve CCTV effectiveness at night. Well-lit perimeters make concealment difficult.
AlarmsDetectiveTrigger when a sensor detects unauthorized access. Can be audible (deter the intruder directly) or silent (notify security remotely without alerting the attacker).
BollardsPreventive / PhysicalShort, sturdy posts installed at building entrances to prevent vehicles from ramming into a facility. Used at banks, government buildings, and critical infrastructure.
Dual gatePreventive / PhysicalA two-gate vehicle entry checkpoint where the second gate cannot open until the first closes and security verifies the vehicle — prevents tailgating at vehicle checkpoints.
Access control vestibulePreventive / PhysicalAlso called a mantrap. Two interlocking doors for foot traffic — only one can be open at a time. Eliminates tailgating into secured areas like data centers.

Security Sensors

Detecting intrusion through different physical phenomena

Sensors monitor the physical environment and trigger alerts or alarms when specific conditions are detected. Different sensor technologies are suited to different environments and threat types.

Sensor TypeDetection MethodBest Used For
Motion detectionDetects movement in a monitored zone — often combines infrared and microwave techniquesGeneral-purpose interior and exterior intrusion detection
Noise detectionTriggers when ambient sound exceeds a threshold — tuned for glass breaking, forced entry, gunshotsDetecting forced entry through windows or walls
Infrared detectionDetects heat signatures (thermal radiation) emitted by people, animals, or warm objectsAfter-hours perimeter monitoring; works in complete darkness
Pressure detectionTriggers when weight is applied to an embedded floor or surface sensorDetecting movement through a secured room or vault floor
Microwave detectionEmits microwave pulses and measures reflections from moving objects (Doppler effect)Large open areas, garages, warehouses; works through walls
Ultrasonic detectionEmits high-frequency sound waves and detects changes in reflection caused by movementIndoor spaces; very sensitive but can produce false positives from HVAC air movement

Physical Attacks

Threats targeting the physical layer

Credit card skimming

Attaching a hidden device to a legitimate payment terminal (ATM, gas pump, POS) that reads and stores magnetic stripe data from cards as they are swiped. The victim and terminal operator usually can't tell the device is present.

Card cloning

Using data captured by a skimmer to produce a duplicate card that behaves like the original. Skimming steals the data; cloning produces the fraudulent physical card used to make purchases.

Physical brute force

Using direct physical force to bypass access controls — breaking locks, ramming doors, smashing windows, or destroying badge readers. Countered by reinforced doors, deadbolts, security-grade glass, and guards.

Environmental attack

Targeting the physical environment that supports IT — cutting power, triggering fire suppression, flooding a data center, or disabling HVAC. Takes systems offline without touching the hardware directly.

Exam framing: if a question describes an attacker disrupting power, cooling, or suppression systems to take systems offline, the answer is environmental attack. If they force a door or break a lock, it is physical brute force.

Asset Management

You can't protect what you don't know you have

Asset management is the systematic process of tracking, maintaining, and protecting all organizational assets from the moment they are acquired until they are disposed of. Assets include hardware, software, and data — each with its own management requirements. Untracked assets create unmanaged risk.

Lifecycle PhaseWhat HappensKey Activities
Acquisition / ProcurementAssets are purchased and received by the organizationVendor vetting, purchase orders, receiving inspection, initial security configuration
Assignment / AccountingAssets are formally assigned to users or systems and recordedSerial number logging, CMDB entry, department assignment, owner accountability
Monitoring / Asset trackingAssets are tracked throughout their operational lifeRFID tags, inventory audits, patch status tracking, location monitoring, usage reporting

Hardware asset management

Tracking physical devices — servers, laptops, switches, printers — by serial number, location, owner, and status. A lost or untracked device is an unmanaged attack surface.

Software asset management

Tracking software licenses, versions, and installation counts to ensure compliance and identify unauthorized, outdated, or unlicensed software across the environment.

Data asset management

Managing data through classification, ownership, retention policies, and secure disposal. Ensures sensitive data is protected appropriately and deleted securely at end of life.

RFID in asset tracking

Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) tags affixed to assets allow inventory systems to automatically read and log asset locations using radio signals — without requiring line-of-sight scanning. Used in data centers, warehouses, and hospitals to track hardware at scale. RFID tags can be passive (no battery, shorter range) or active (battery-powered, longer range).

Asset Management Vulnerabilities

What happens when asset management fails

Architecture and design weaknesses

Security flaws baked into a system's fundamental design rather than introduced later. These are the hardest to fix because remediation often requires redesigning or replacing the system entirely, not just patching it.

System sprawl

Uncontrolled growth of systems, servers, and VMs without adequate tracking, documentation, or governance. Sprawling environments have unpatched machines, unknown services, and expanded attack surfaces that security teams can't keep up with.

Sprawl risk: every untracked or undocumented system is a potential entry point. Asset management isn't just an accounting exercise — it directly determines what you can patch, monitor, and defend.

Defense in Depth

Layer controls so no single failure compromises everything

Defense in depth is the strategy of applying multiple, overlapping layers of security controls so that if one layer fails or is bypassed, others remain in place to limit the damage. No single control is assumed to be perfect — the combination is what makes the system resilient.

Vendor diversity

Using products from multiple vendors across security layers. A zero-day vulnerability in one vendor's product doesn't compromise every layer simultaneously. If your firewall and endpoint protection come from different vendors, a flaw in one leaves the other intact.

Technology diversity

Deploying different technologies at different layers. An attacker exploiting a Windows-specific flaw won't automatically own Linux-based systems. Different protocols, platforms, and architectures require different attack techniques.

Control diversity

Applying a mix of control types — preventive (stop the attack), detective (identify it), corrective (fix the damage), and deterrent (discourage attempts). If a preventive control is bypassed, detective controls still catch it.

Exam framing: when a question asks why an organization uses multiple vendors or control types, the answer is defense in depth. The sub-strategy being used (vendor vs. technology vs. control diversity) depends on what's being varied.

RAID & Storage Redundancy

Protect data availability at the disk level

RAID (Redundant Array of Inexpensive Disks) combines multiple physical drives into a single logical unit to improve performance, redundancy, or both. RAID protects against physical drive failure — it does not protect against deletion, corruption, or ransomware. RAID is not a backup.

RAID LevelMethodMin DrivesDrives That Can FailTrade-off
RAID-0Striping — data split across all drives in chunks20 — any failure = total data lossMaximum performance + capacity; zero fault tolerance
RAID-1Mirroring — identical copy on every drive21 (the other drive has a full copy)Simple redundancy; uses 50% of total capacity
RAID-5Striping + distributed parity (1 parity block per stripe)31Good balance of speed, capacity, and redundancy; rebuild required after failure
RAID-6Striping + double distributed parity (2 parity blocks per stripe)42 simultaneouslyHigher fault tolerance than RAID-5; slower writes due to double parity calculation
RAID-10Mirror + stripe (RAID 1+0): data mirrored across pairs, then striped across pairs41 per mirror pair (potentially multiple)High performance AND high redundancy; expensive — 50% capacity usable
RAID ≠ Backup: RAID protects against hardware drive failure only. It does not protect against accidental deletion, ransomware encryption, fire, flood, or software corruption. A deleted file is deleted on all mirror drives instantly. Always back up RAID arrays separately.

High Availability & Power

Eliminate single points of failure at every layer

High availability (HA) is the design goal of keeping systems running with minimal downtime by eliminating single points of failure through redundancy and automatic failover. HA applies to servers, network connections, applications, and power — any failure point must have a backup ready to take over.

HA TechniqueHow It WorksKey Distinction
Active/active load balancingAll nodes handle live traffic simultaneously — load is distributed across every nodeMaximum throughput; all capacity in use; any node can fail without service interruption
Active/passive load balancingOne or more standby nodes sit idle until an active node fails, then take overLower utilization in normal operation; dedicated failover capacity always available
Software load balancerLoad balancing logic runs as software or a virtual appliance rather than dedicated hardwareFlexible, cost-effective, easily scaled; examples include HAProxy, NGINX, and cloud-native LBs
ClusteringMultiple servers grouped into a single logical unit — they appear as one system and share workloads and stateProvides both HA and scale-out; if one node fails, others continue serving the cluster workload
NIC teamingTwo or more physical network interface cards bonded into one logical interfaceIf one NIC or cable fails, others maintain connectivity; can also increase aggregate bandwidth

Power Redundancy

Keep the lights on at every tier

Power failures cascade: no power means no servers, no cooling, no networking. Power redundancy protects at three tiers — the utility feed, the facility, and the individual server.

DeviceWhat It DoesDuration / Scope
UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply)Battery-backed device that provides immediate power the moment utility power fails — no gap between outage and power deliveryMinutes to ~1 hour depending on load; buys time for graceful shutdown or generator start
Dual power supplyTwo independent PSUs inside a single server, each connected to a different circuit or UPSServer stays on even if one PSU or one power circuit fails
GeneratorFuel-powered device that produces electricity to sustain a facility during extended outages after the UPS battery depletesHours to days depending on fuel supply; starts in seconds to minutes after outage
Managed PDUSmart power strip that distributes power to rack equipment with remote monitoring, per-outlet switching, and consumption trackingAllows remote power cycling of hung servers without physical access to the data center
Power protection layers: UPS handles short outages and spikes (seconds to minutes). The generator kicks in for extended outages (hours+). Dual PSU handles component-level power failures inside a server. All three together eliminate power as a single point of failure.

Backups & Data Protection

The last line of defense against data loss

Backups are copies of data preserved so that original data can be restored after a loss event. Backup strategy involves choosing the right media, backup type, frequency, and storage location — balancing storage cost, restore speed, and recovery objectives (RTO and RPO).

Backup Media

Where you store your backups
Media TypeDescriptionBest For
TapeSequential magnetic media — high capacity, low cost per GB, long archival lifeLong-term archival, regulatory compliance data retention
DiskSpinning or solid-state drives — fast random access, immediate restoreOperational backups requiring fast restore
NAS (Network Attached Storage)File-level storage device shared over the network — multiple clients write backups to a central NASCentralized backup target for multiple systems
SAN (Storage Area Network)Block-level storage over a dedicated high-speed network — appears to the OS as a local driveEnterprise backup and replication at scale
Cloud storageData stored on remote servers managed by a cloud provider — geographically distributed, scalableOffsite backup, disaster recovery, geographic redundancy
Online backupBackup data immediately accessible for restore — connected to the networkFast restore; note: connected backups are vulnerable to ransomware
Offline backupBackup data on physically disconnected media (tape, removable disk, air-gapped vault)Ransomware protection; immune to network-based attacks
Offsite backupCopies stored at a geographically separate location from the primary siteProtection against site-wide disasters (fire, flood, power failure)

The 3-2-1 Backup Rule

Keep 3 copies of data, on 2 different media types, with 1 copy stored offsite. This single rule simultaneously protects against media failure (two types), local disasters (offsite copy), and single points of failure (multiple copies). Every enterprise backup strategy should implement at minimum the 3-2-1 rule.

Backup Types

What gets backed up each time
TypeWhat Gets Backed UpStorage UsedRestore RequirementRestore Speed
Full backupEverything — a complete copy of all selected dataHighest (entire dataset each time)This backup onlyFastest
Differential backupAll changes since the last full backup (cumulative — grows over time)Medium (grows as the week progresses)Last full + latest differentialFast (2 backup sets)
Incremental backupOnly changes since the last backup of any typeLowest (smallest each night)Last full + every incremental sinceSlowest
Snapshot backupA point-in-time copy of the system state — often at the hypervisor or storage layerVaries (only changed blocks after initial)Snapshot image onlyVery fast (near-instant rollback)

Replication

Continuously or near-continuously copying data to a secondary location so the replica stays synchronized with the source. Synchronous replication (zero data loss) and asynchronous replication (small delay, less impact on performance). Supports near-zero RPO when done synchronously.

Journaling

Recording every change to a database or file system as a sequential transaction log (journal). If a crash occurs, the journal is replayed from the last known good state to restore consistency. Databases use journaling to prevent partial writes from corrupting data.

Backup comparison shortcut: Full = most storage, fastest restore. Incremental = least storage, slowest restore. Differential = middle ground (2 backup sets to restore). Snapshot = fastest for rollback, typically short-term only.

Database Backup Context: Hot, Warm, and Cold

These terms describe database state during backup — NOT disaster recovery sites
Critical distinction: Hot, warm, and cold are used in two completely different contexts on the Security+ exam — database backup types (this section) and disaster recovery sites (covered in the Recovery section). Do not confuse them. A "hot backup" is not the same as a "hot site."
Database Backup TypeDatabase State During BackupTrade-off
Hot backup (database)Database is fully online and serving transactions — users are unaffected during backupRequires special tooling to ensure consistency (since data can change during the backup); no downtime
Warm backup (database)Database is partially offline — some services or connections are paused but a full shutdown is not requiredBalance between consistency and availability; some brief degradation to users
Cold backup (database)Database is completely shut down — no transactions possible during backupMost consistent backup (no changes possible during copy) but requires scheduled downtime

Business Continuity Planning

Keep the business running when everything goes wrong

A Business Continuity Plan (BCP) is the comprehensive strategy for maintaining or rapidly resuming critical business functions during and after a disruption. While a Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP) focuses on restoring IT systems, a BCP covers the entire organization — people, processes, facilities, and technology.

Types of Disasters

Knowing the threat shapes the plan
Disaster TypeExamplesPlanning Focus
Environmental disastersHurricanes, earthquakes, floods, wildfires, ice stormsGeographic redundancy, physical hardening, evacuation procedures
Human-made disastersCyberattacks, terrorism, sabotage, civil unrest, deliberate data destructionSecurity controls, incident response, insider threat programs
Internal disastersHardware failure, software bugs, accidental data deletion, power outages, insider mistakesRedundancy, change management, testing, employee training
External disastersVendor failure, utility outage, supply chain disruption, regulatory shutdownVendor contracts, SLAs, alternative supplier identification

Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

Identify what matters most before a disaster strikes

A Business Impact Analysis (BIA) is the process of identifying mission-essential functions — the critical activities that must continue during a disruption — and quantifying the impact if those functions are disrupted. The BIA drives every other decision in the BCP.

Mission Essential Functions

The subset of business processes that are absolutely critical to the organization's survival or legal obligations. Identified during the BIA, these functions receive the highest priority in recovery plans and determine RTO/RPO targets. For a hospital, this includes patient care systems. For a bank, core transaction processing.

BIA Impact Scenarios

Quantifying what failure actually means
Impact CategoryWhat It MeasuresExamples
Life lossPhysical danger or fatality risk to personnel, patients, or the publicHospital systems down → delayed treatment; industrial control failure → equipment hazard
Loss of propertyDamage, destruction, or theft of physical or digital assetsServer room flooding, ransomware destroying data, theft of equipment
Safety reductionDegraded safety systems, emergency response capability, or working conditionsFire suppression failure, disabled alarm systems, loss of emergency communications
Financial lossesDirect revenue loss, regulatory fines, recovery costs, contract penaltiesE-commerce downtime, GDPR fines, breach notification costs, SLA penalties
Reputation lossesBrand damage, customer trust erosion, media coverage, client attritionPublic data breach, prolonged service outage, visible security failure

Continuity of Operations Planning (COOP)

A plan that ensures mission-essential functions continue from an alternate location or with alternate staff when the primary facility is unavailable. COOP is a specific implementation of BCP concepts — it pre-identifies alternate sites, cross-trained personnel, and the procedures to activate remote operations quickly. Commonly associated with government agencies but applicable to any organization.

Recovery Metrics & Disaster Recovery

Measure recovery readiness before the disaster, not after

Key Recovery Metrics

The numbers that drive your DR design
MetricFull NameMeasuresWho Sets It
RTORecovery Time ObjectiveMaximum acceptable time a system can be offline — "how long can we be down?"Business requirement (driven by revenue/safety impact)
RPORecovery Point ObjectiveMaximum acceptable data loss measured in time — "how far back can we restore?"Business requirement (driven by data criticality)
MTBFMean Time Between FailuresAverage operational time between failures — reliability of a component or systemEngineering / vendor specification
MTTRMean Time To RepairAverage time to restore a failed system to operation — repair speedOperations / SLA targets
Memory shortcut: RPO = data loss tolerance (measured by how long ago you backed up). RTO = downtime tolerance (how long you can be offline). MTBF tells you how often something fails. MTTR tells you how long it takes to fix it.

Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP)

The IT-focused plan for restoring systems after a disaster

A Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP) focuses specifically on restoring IT systems, data, and infrastructure after a disruption. It documents recovery procedures, responsibilities, communication plans, and the recovery sites to which operations will move.

Recovery Sites & Site Resiliency

Where operations go when the primary site fails
Context matters: Hot, warm, cold, and mobile site refer to disaster recovery sites — not to be confused with hot/warm/cold database backup types covered in the Backups section.
Site TypeReadinessRecovery TimeCost
Hot siteFully operational with live, up-to-date data and hardware mirroring productionMinutes to hoursHighest (running full duplicate infrastructure)
Warm siteHardware in place and configured, but data must be restored from backup before going liveHours to daysMedium
Cold siteBasic utilities only (power, network) — no equipment, no dataDays to weeksLowest (empty facility)
Mobile sitePortable facility (trailer, container) deployed to any location; pre-loaded or rapidly configuredHours to days (deployment dependent)Varies; good when fixed sites are impractical

Site Resiliency

The overall capability of an organization's recovery infrastructure to sustain operations and recover quickly when the primary site fails. High site resiliency means the DR site can handle production load immediately (hot) or with minimal preparation. Low resiliency means long activation times and data gaps (cold).

Restoration

The process of returning systems and data to normal operation at the primary site after the disaster has passed and the site is repaired. Restoration follows recovery — recovery gets you operational at the DR site; restoration brings you back home.

DR Testing Methods

Verify the plan works before you need it
Test TypeWhat HappensRisk to ProductionRealism
Tabletop exerciseTeam gathers to verbally discuss how they would respond to a scenario — no systems touchedNoneLow (discussion only)
SimulationsTeam actively responds to a realistic mock scenario — actions taken but production not cut overLowMedium
Parallel processingRecovery system runs alongside production simultaneously; outputs compared before cutoverLow (both systems running)High (real workload on recovery system)
Failover testProduction traffic actually switches to the recovery system — a real cutover with real consequences if something failsHighHighest (identical to a real disaster)
Test progression: start with tabletop (safest), move to simulations, then parallel processing, then a full failover test (most realistic but most disruptive). Each level reveals gaps the previous one misses.

Capacity Planning

Plan for tomorrow's load today

Capacity planning is the process of proactively determining whether an organization will have sufficient resources to handle current and future workload demands. Unlike BCP (which plans for disruptions), capacity planning addresses normal growth — ensuring you don't run out of servers, staff, or space because the business grew faster than infrastructure.

People capacity

Do you have enough trained personnel to handle peak operational loads, support growth, cover absences, and respond to incidents? Understaffed security teams create coverage gaps that attackers exploit. Cross-training and succession planning are key.

Technology capacity

Are systems, compute, storage, and software licenses scalable to meet projected demand? This includes server CPU/RAM headroom, storage growth rates, network bandwidth, and cloud resource limits. Over-provisioned systems waste money; under-provisioned systems fail under load.

Infrastructure capacity

Is the physical and network infrastructure sufficient for growth? Data center floor space, power capacity, cooling (HVAC) tonnage, and network bandwidth must be planned ahead of demand — adding a rack or cooling unit takes time. Cloud can offload some infrastructure scaling.

Exam framing: capacity planning is proactive growth planning, not incident response. If a question describes analyzing future needs for staff, servers, or infrastructure — the answer is capacity planning.
